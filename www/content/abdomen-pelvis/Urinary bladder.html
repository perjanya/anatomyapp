Urinary bladder

Location

Anterior part of lesser pelvis

Behind pubic symphysis

In front of rectum in males 

In front of uterus in females

Expansion into abdominal cavity

Empty bladder is completely pelvic

Distended bladder enlarges into the infraumbilical part of abdomen

Bladder distension

It is customary to evaluate the transabdominal pelvic ultrasonography with distended bladder. Distended bladder acts as an acoustic window

However, transvaginal ultrasonography can be equally effective in detecting pelvic organs in females, without compulsorily subjecting every patient for bladder distension

  2.  Two inferolateral surfaces
  3. One inferoposterior surface (posterior, base)

Trigone of bladder

Mucosa is firmly bound to underlying muscular coat - makes trigone smooth

Trigone of bladder – boundaries

Interureteric ridge on top

Uretero - urethral ridge on either side

Trigone of bladder – development

From absorbed portion of caudal end of mesonephric duct

Mesonephric duct is mesodermal in origin

Rest of the bladder develops from primitive urogenital sinus, endodermal origin

Early in development, trigone of bladder is mesodermal in origin. Later - the endodermal epithelium forms the mucosa of trigone of bladder

This concept is recently challenged. There is limited evidence for the same. Using cre-lox recombination lineage system in mice, trigone of bladder has same origin as that of bladder. Ref: (Development 2007 134: 3763-3769; doi: 10.1242/dev.011270)

Trigone of bladder - wall architecture

Detrussor muscle in the rest of bladder is arranged in whorls and spirals in a single layer

In trigone of bladder detrussor has well defined three layers - inner longitudinal, middle circular, outer longitudinal

Additionally, the trigone has muscles essentially continuing with ureters.

This is divided into two layers.

Superficial layer

  • Longitudinal fibres of the intravesical ureter at the ureterovesical orifice that spread into the base of urinary bladder

Deep layer -

  • Continuation of the fibromuscular tissue around the terminal part of the ureters (sheath of Waldeyer)

Submucosa is not demonstrable in trigone of bladder. Mucosa is directly adherent to muscular coat

Trigone of bladder – physiology

It is relatively indistensible.

The exact function of the trigone in the act of micturition is not yet known.

(Ref: Shafik A, Role of trigone in micturition. J Endourol. 1998 Jun;12(3):273-7)

Innervation of bladder

The central nervous regulation of the lower urinary tract is mediated by simple on-off switching circuits in the brain and spinal cord that are under voluntary control

Innervation – Sympathetic

  • Intermediolateral cell column at T11-L2 or at L1 and L2
  • Lumbar splanchnic nerves
  • Superior hypogastric plexus
  • Right and left hypogastric nerves
  • Right and left inferior pelvic hypogastric plexuses
  • Autonomic innervation of the superficial trigone musculature and the urethra

Innervation – Parasympathetic

  • Intermediate gray of the spinal cord at S2, S3, and S4
  • Pelvic splanchnic nerves (or nervi erigentes)
  • Right and left pelvic plexuses
  • Preganglionic fibers ultimately synapse in ganglia in the pelvic connective tissues or upon, or within, the walls of the pelvic organs
  • Provide motor stimulation for the detrusor musculature of the bladder

Innervation - sensory fibres

  • Sacral part of the spinal cord
  • Pelvic splanchnic nerves
  • Sympathetic pathways up through the pelvic, hypogastric, and preaortic plexuses to reach the sympathetic chains
  • Duality of the pathways for pain
  • Presacral neurectomy does not materially reduce vesical pain
  • Bilateral anterolateral cordotomy may be required

Innervation - somatic fibres

  • Sacral cord at S2, S3, S4
  • Pudendal nerve
  • Innervation of the periurethral striated sphincteric apparatus that voluntarily stops urination
  • Compressor urethrae muscle and the sphincter urethrae of the urogenital diaphragm and the bulbospongiosus muscle of the superficial perineal compartment

Innervation - cortical representation

  • Paracentral lobule
  • Evoke bladder contraction

Micturition

Act of voiding

Fundamentally a spinal reflex facilitated and inhibited by higher centers

Storage reflex

  • Slow filling of bladder, produce slow distention of bladder
  • This stimulates sympathetic outflow in
    • Hypogastric nerves - to bladder outlet
    • Pudendal nerves - to external urethral sphincter
    • Inhibits contraction of detrussor muscle
  • Rostral pontine storage center - increase urethral storage activity

Voiding reflex

  • Spinobulbospinal reflex pathways - pass through the pontine micturition centre
  • Stimulates the parasympathetic outflow to the bladder and to urethral smooth muscle
  • Inhibits the sympathetic and pudendal outflow to the urethral outlet
    • Periaqueductal grey relay impulses before reaching the pontine micturition centre

Vesicoureteral reflux

Factors preventing vesicoureteral reflux

  • Oblique course of ureters within the bladder musculature and submucosally
  • Ureteral muscles blends with detrusor muscles posteriorly
  • These factors result in valve like mechanism around the intramural part of ureter

This prevents reflux of urine back into the ureters during normal filling and more so during micturition

Effects of vesicoureteral reflux

This leads to Megaloureter, hydroureter later may cause hydronephrosis
