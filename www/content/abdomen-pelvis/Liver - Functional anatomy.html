Liver - functional anatomy

Etymology

Old English lifer, of Germanic origin; related to German Leber, Dutch lever

Location

In Right Upper quadrant

In Right hypochondrium extending into the epigastrium

Largest solid organ

Liver is the largest solid organ in body

Liver weighs 1.4 to 1.6 Kg.

Largest organ is the skin

Movement

Liver is located just below the diaphragm

It moves with respiration

All structures related (connected) to diaphragm moves with respiration

Anatomical Lobes

Anatomical lobes of liver

Falciform ligament divides into - 

Right lobe - large lobe on the right side of falciform ligament

Left lobe - small lobe on the left side of falciform ligament

Viewed from inferior surface - 

Caudate lobe

Quadrate lobe

Functional lobes

Cystocaval line - Cantlie line

Liver is divided into equal right and left lobes by a line joining gall bladder and inferior vena cava. 

Right lobe

  • Roughly six times larger than left lobe
  • Occupies right hypochondrium
  • Anteriorly - on the right side of falciform ligament
  • Inferiorly and posteriorly - on the right side of ligamentum venosum and ligamentum teres
  • Middle hepatic vein separates right lobe and left lobe functionally

Left lobe

  • Small and flat
  • Located in epigastrium
  • Superiorly related to diaphragm
  • Inferiorly related to stomach

Quadrate lobe

Boundaries

  • Anterior - inferior border of liver
  • posterior - porta hepatis
  • Right side - fossa for gall bladder
  • Left side - Ligamentum teres

Quadrate lobe is Couinaud segment IVb

Caudate lobe

Synonyms - posterior hepatic segment, Spigelian lobe

Location- Posterior-superior aspect of right lobe of liver

Boundaries

  • Inferior - porta hepatis
  • Right - fossa for inferior vena cava
  • Left - ligamentum venosum

Blood from caudate lobe drains directly into the inferior vena cava

Couinaud's classification

Division of liver into 8 segments based on vascular inflow, outflow and bile drainage

Each Couinaud's segment has its own branch of portal vein, hepatic artery and bile duct in the center. 

Each Couinaud's segment is drained by a tributary of hepatic vein in the periphery

Numbering of the segments is in clockwise manner

Segment 1  - located posteriorly. Caudate lobe is segment 1

Segment 2 - in the upper part of left lobe

Segment 3 - in lower part of left lobe

Segment 4 - On the left of middle hepatic vein (in anatomical right lobe; physiological left lobe)

Segment 5 - in lower part of right lobe (anterior aspect)

Segment 6 - in lower part of right lobe (posterior aspect)

Segment 7 - in upper part of right lobe (posterior aspect)

Segment 8 - in upper part of right lobe (anterior aspect)

Segment 1 to 4 supplied by left branch of hepatic artery, left tributary of hepatic vein and drained by left haptic duct

Segment 5 to 8 supplied by right branch of hepatic artery, right tributary of hepatic vein and drained by right haptic duct

Inferior border

At the junction of diaphragmatic and visceral surfaces

Thin and sharp

Extends from 9th costal margin on right side to 8th costal margin on the left side

On deep inspiration - Inferior border is felt during per abdominal examination

Surfaces

Diaphragmatic surface

  • Indistinct subdivisions - Superior, anterior, right lateral and posterior surfaces
  • Bare area of liver
  • Fissure for ligamentum venosum
  • Attachment of coronary ligaments, right and left triangular ligament and falciform ligament

Visceral surface

  • Has porta hepatis, fossa for gall bladder and fissure for ligamentum teres hepatis

Important relations

Visceral surface

  • From left to right
    • Stomach - gastric impression
    • Fissure for ligamentum teres
    • On right side - quadrate lobe
    • Fossa for gall bladder
    • Right colic flexure
    • Junction of first and second part of duadenum
    • Right kidney (hepatorenal pouch of Morison) and Right suprarenal gland

Posterior surface

  • Bare area of liver - direct contact with diaphragm
  • Caudate lobe - related to lesser sac
  • Left lobe - related to abdominal esophagus

Diaphragmatic surface

  • Superior surface - central tendon of diaphragm
  • Anterior surface - falciform ligament
  • Right lateral surface - Upper part related to lung and pleura, middle part to costodiaphragmatic recess (only to pleura)

Liver biopsy: Needle is inserted into the mid-axillary line in 9th or 10th intercostal space

Blood supply

Arterial supply - Two sources

  1. Arterial blood from hepatic artery (20%)
  2. Venous blood via portal vein (80 %)

Venous drainage

Hepatic veins drain to inferior vena cava

Ligamentum teres hepatis

Present within the free margin of falciform ligament

Represent the remnant of left umbilical vein

Ligamentum venosum

Fibrous remnant of the ductus venosus of the fetal circulation

Attached to the left branch of the portal vein within the porta hepatis

Bare area of liver

  • Non-peritoneal triangular area on the posterior surface of the liver
  • Related to diaphragm
  • Vein within the liver communicate with diaphragmatic veins - forms the important site of portocaval anastomosis
  • Infection and tumors can spread (outside the liver) faster through this surface

Hepatorenal pouch of Morison

  • Space between liver and right kidney

Most dependent portion of the peritoneum in the upper abdomen in supine position

Clinical implications

  • Inferior border of liver can be palpated during abdominal examination
  • Palpating inferior border of liver - can be either due to enlarged liver or pushed down liver
  • Enlarged liver is confirmed by liver span (percussion finding)
  • Liver biopsy is done along mid-axillary line in 9th or 10th intercostal space
