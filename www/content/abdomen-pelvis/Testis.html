Testis

Etymology

Testis - from Latin, literally ‘a witness’ (i.e. to virility)

Testis - pleural; Testes - singular

Other names - testicle

The words - orchido, orchid, orchio, orchi, orchium, orchido and didumos refers to testis.

In ancient Greece, orchids were associated with virility and it was believed that if the father ate orchid tubers, the baby would be boy and if mother ate, the baby would be girl.

Location

Testis is located within the scrotum; left testis is lower than the right.

Interesting point

Testis of elephants, platypus (monotremes), tree sloths and armadillos (xenarthra) are located within the abdomen.

Reason for external testis

Activation hypothesis:

Lower temperatures as happen in externally placed testis in scrotum prevent premature activation of the sperms. When sperms enters the cervix, increased temperature enable them with increased motility to reach uterine tube.

Externally placed testes are more pain sensitive. Sensitivity to pain leads to extra care of the testes from damage.

For more information refer Gallup. https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/pdf/10.1177/147470490900700402

Endothermic pulse hypothesis:

Lower body temperatures prevailed in Cenozoic era. During evolution, as an adaptation to the climate change and to cursoriality (ability to run) led to increased body temperature. This led to externally placed testis at a lower temperature.

For more information refer Lovegrove. https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/jeb.12373

Size and measurement

Adult testis measures around 4 cm in length

Orchidometer - is used to measure the size of the testis. However, in modern days - radiological evaluation measures it more accurately.

Coverings

Tunica vaginalis

Tunica albuginea

Tunica vasculosa

The tunica vaginalis

Covers the testis on all sides except on its posterior border and superior pole

Vaginal process of the peritoneum

Has two layers

  • Visceral layer - covers testis and epididymis
  • Parietal layer - extends to some part of spermatic cord

Cavum vaginale - cavity between the two layers of the tunica vaginalis

The tunica albuginea

Dense, irregular connective tissue capsule of the seminiferous tubules

Modified into mediastinum testis in the midline

From these connective septa divide the testis into 200 to 300 compartments

1 to 4 seminiferous tubules are located within these compartments

These tubules anastomose in the mediastinum of testis - forms rete testis

6 to 10 ducts from this enters the head of epididymis

Tunica vasculosa

Vascular layer of testis

Consists of loose areolar tissue with delicate blood vessels

Parts of testis

Surfaces - medial and lateral

Borders - anterior and posterior

Head of epididymis is related to upper part of posterior border

Body and tail of epididymis related to lower part of the posterior border

Seminiferous tubules

Lined by multilayered epithelium

Each tubules have 150-250 microns diameter

The average total length in each testis is 540 m (range 299-981 m)

Most mature cells located towards the lumen

Contain Sertoli cells and spermatogonia cells

Immature tubules are positive for alpha inhibin

Spermatogonia cells

Include spermatogonia, primary spermatocytes, secondary spermatocytes, spermatids and spermatozoa

Spermatogonia have type A and type B subgroups.

Type A has pale cells (type Ap cells) and dark cells (type Ad cells)

Type Ad are reservoir cells

Type B cell division gives rise to primary spermatocytes in a process of spermatocytogenesis.

Except spermatozoa all other speratogonial cells are held together by a narrow cytoplasmic bridge

Leydig cells

In between these tubules Leydig cells are embedded in interstitial connective tissue

Leydig cells secrete - testosterone, androstenedione and dehydroepiandrosterone

Luteinizing hormone (LH) stimulated Leydig cells for their secretion

Seminiferous tubules are covered outside by collagen fibres with myoid cells

Sertoli cells

Sertoli cells have irregular, highly folded nuclei with prominent nucleoli

Sertoli cells are trophic to the spermatogonial cells

Secrete androgen-binding protein and are responsive to FSH

Also produce inhibin

Age changes

  • Birth to age 4 (static phase): seminiferous tubules filled with small cuboidal cells with no definite lumen present, Leydig cells usually not visible
  • Age 4-10 (growth phase): tubules, tubular lumina and cells enlarge; tubules become tortuous
  • Age 10 to puberty (maturation phase): tubular cells have mitoses; Leydig cells prominent; spermatocyte differentiation visible
  • Adult (post-puberty): each testis weights 15-19g, measures 5 x 3 cm; takes 70 days for cells to mature from spermatogonium to primary spermatocyte to secondary spermatocyte to spermatid to spermatozoa; maturation is orderly along length of tubule

Arterial supply of testis and other scrotal structures

Testicular artery - branch of abdominal aorta (developmentally testis is abdominal organ, later descends into the scrotum)

It supplies both testis and epididymis

Ductus deferens is supplied by artery of ductus deferens (branch of inferior vesicular artery)

Cremasteric artery supplies cremasteric fascia, muscle and other nearby structures. It arises from inferior epigastric artery

Good anastomoses exist between these arteries

Other arteries reinforcing scrotal blood supply are

1. Anterior scrotal artery - branches of the deep external pudendal artery

2. Posterior scrotal artery - branches of the internal pudendal artery

Venous drainage

Pampiniform plexus

Formed within the spermatic cord by 10 to 12 veins from testis

These veins form anterior and posterior groups.

Progressively veins join to form bigger veins.

At the deep inguinal ring - usually two veins are present

The vein on the right side - drain into inferior vena cava

The vein on the left side - drain into the left renal vein (opposite the drainage of left suprarenal vein)

Cremasteric veins drains into the inferior epigastric vein

Deferential vein drains into the pelvic plexus

Lymphatics

Lymphatics from testis and the epididymis drain upward through the spermatic cord.

They drain to the lateral and preaortic lymph nodes

Innervation

Sympathetic innervation: from T10 to T12 through celiac and testicular plexus

General visceral sensory fibers reach T10 to T12 travel through the same plexus and through lesser and least splanchnic nerves

Cremaster - supplied by genital branch of genitofemoral nerve

Ilioinguinal nerve (L1) - enters the inguinal canal after passing between the external oblique muscle and internal oblique muscle emerge through the superficial inguinal ring

Supplies - skin of the penile root, the upper part of the scrotum, and the upper, medial thigh

Applied aspects – varicocele

Enlargement of veins within the scrotum is varicocele

Large varicocele may appear as bag of worms upon palpation

Left side varicocele is more common

Anatomical reasons

1. Left testicular vein enters left renal vein at right angle. Exactly opposite to this, the left suprarenal vein drains into the left renal vein carrying adrenergic hormones from the suprarenal medulla

2. Left testicular vein is compressed by loaded sigmoid colon in the left iliac fossa

3. left testis is more lower than the right side

Applied aspects - testicular torsion

Twisting of spermatic cord

Leading to cutting off the blood supply to testis

Patient suffers from excruciating pain

Cremasteric reflex is absent

Prolonged ischemia may interfere with sperm production

It’s a surgical emergency

Immediate surgical intervention to restore the blood supply can salvage the testis
