Female external genitalia

Mons pubis

Hair bearing elevated area filled with fat present over the pubic symphysis

Labia majora

Fat filled fold of skin on either side of the vulva. Hair +

Termination of round ligament of uterus

Clinical relevance

  • Site for:
    • Bartholin cyst extension
    • Vulvar carcinoma
  • Swelling → can indicate inguinal hernia (canal of Nuck)

Practical relevance

  • Provide mechanical protection and maintain moisture balance

Labia minora

  • Smooth, hairless folds of skin
  • Anterior ends splits to form two folds
  • Anterior fold - fuse with inferior part of clitoris
  • Posterior fold - form prepuce over the tip of the clitoris

Pudendal cleft: Space between the two labia majora

Highly vascular and innervated → key role in sexual arousal and Rich in sebaceous glands

Clinical relevance

  • Hypertrophy → labiaplasty (cosmetic surgery)
  • Tears during childbirth → perineal repair importance

Practical relevance

  • Maintain vestibular environment (lubrication + protection)

Vestibule of vagina

Space between the labia minora

Has urethral orifice, vaginal orifice and openings of greater vestibular glands

Clinical relevance

  • Bartholin cyst/abscess → painful swelling
  • Site for:
    • Urinary infections
    • Sexually transmitted infections

Practical relevance

  • Important in:
    • Urination control
    • Sexual function

Clitoris

Erectile tissue measuring 2 -3 cm

Consists of two crura (covered by ischiocavernosus muscle)  and two corpus cavernosa

Covered by a sensitive tubercle (glans)

Vaginal orifice

Orifice is surrounded by hymen.

Ducts of greater vestibular glands (Bartholin’s glands) are located on either side of the vestibule

Hymen is variable in shape and elasticity. Not a reliable indicator of virginity

Clinical relevance

  • Imperforate hymen → hematocolpos
  • Tear during childbirth → normal finding

Urethral orifice

Located immediately anterior to the vaginal orifice

Skene glands (paraurethral glands) are located on its posterior aspect

Bartholin’s (Greater Vestibular) Glands

Located posterolaterally

Clinical relevance

  • Bartholin cyst / abscess
  • Treatment: Marsupialization

Vestibular bulbs

  • Erectile tissues surrounding the vestibule
  • Located under the bulbocavernosus muscle
  • Equivalent to corpus spongiosum in males
  • Part of extended clitoral complex

Clinical relevance

Injury → bleeding during childbirth

Important in sexual arousal physiology

Muscles

Ichiocavernosus

  • Origin: ischial tuberosity
  • Insertion: ischiopubic bone
  • Action: compress the underlying crura and lowers the clitoris

Bulbocavernosus

  • Origin: perineal body
  • Insertion: posterior part of clitoris
  • Action: compresses the vestibular bulb and dorsal vein of the clitoris

Neurovascular supply importance

  • Pudendal nerve (S2–S4)
  • Injury → loss of:
    • Sensation
    • Sexual function
    • Continence

G-Spot (Gräfenberg Spot)

  • Described as a sensitive area on the anterior vaginal wall
  • G-spot is a functional zone, not a discrete organ
  • Located ~2–3 cm inside the vagina, posterior to the urethra
  • Not a distinct anatomical structure in all individuals
  • Correspond to Clitoral–urethral–vaginal complex (CUV complex)
  • Rich network of - clitoral internal bulbs, paraurethral (Skene) glands and nerve endings

Clinical relevance

  • Important in:
    • Sexual dysfunction evaluation
    • Counseling patients
    • Explains variation in sexual response

Female Prostate (Skene’s Glands)

  • Paraurethral glands homologous to male prostate

Functions

  • Secrete fluid
  • Possibly involved in female ejaculation

Clinical relevance: Can develop infection and cysts

  • PSA (prostate-specific antigen) detected in females → supports homology

Female Ejaculation

Expulsion of fluid during sexual arousal/orgasm

Recognized but highly variable

Sources

  • Skene’s glands
  • Bladder (in some cases)

Clinical relevance: Must be distinguished from: Urinary incontinence
