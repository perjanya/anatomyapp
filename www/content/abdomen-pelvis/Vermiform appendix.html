Vermiform appendix

Narrow worm-like diverticulum attached to caecum

Lies in right iliac fossa

  • Parts –
    • Base – attached to caecum
      • The base always arises from the cecum at the convergence of the taeniae
    • Body
    • Tip – least vascular

Surface anatomy

McBurney’s point

Junction of medial 2/3rd and lateral 1/3rd of spino-umbilical line

Positions

  • Most common – retrocaecal/retrocolic
  • Other positions – paracolic, splenic, pre and post ileal, promonteric and pelvic

Identification during surgery

Appendix is identified during surgery by tracing one of the taeniae coli down to the base of the appendix. The base of appendix always arise from convergence of taenia coli.

Relations

  • Posteriorly to the iliopsoas muscle and the lumbar plexus of nerves
  • Anteriorly to abdominal wall, the greater omentum, or coils of ileum

Appendicular orifice

  • 2 cm below the ileocaecal orifice
  • Guarded by valve of Gerlach

Arterial supply

Artery of Seshachalam – one of the named accessary appendicular artery

Artery of Seshachalam - only structure in the human body having Indian eponym

Lymphatic drainage

Drain to ileocaecal lymph nodes and in turn to superior mesenteric groups of pre-aortic lymph nodes

Nerve supply

  • Sympathetic innervation of the appendix originates from the celiac and superior mesenteric ganglia.
  • Parasympathetic innervation originates from the vagus nerve.
  • Sensory innervation for pain is carried by the eighth thoracic spinal nerve, or perhaps by the 10th and 11th thoracic nerves

Pain of appendicitis

Initial pain of appendicitis is referred to umbilical region because both have same segmental innervation from T10

Applied anatomy

  • Inflammation of appendix – appendicitis
  • The convergence of the taeniae coli at the appendiceal base will help the surgeon find a hidden appendix
  • Right psoas muscle test: The forced extension of the right thigh produces increased pain in the RLQ of the abdomen when the inflamed appendix and its short mesentery rest on the peritoneum which covers the right major psoas muscle
  • Right obturator muscle test: Flexion and lateral rotation of the right thigh produces increased pain in the RLQ and right pelvic area when the inflamed appendix is closely related to the obturator internus muscle
  • Each position of the appendix produces and mimics a different clinical picture:
    • Retrocecal appendix: RLQ or right flank pain with ureteric irritation
    • Pelvic appendix: Pelvic pain with urinary symptoms; rule out pelvic inflammatory disease
    • Subhepatic appendix: Due to cecal malrotation; presents gallbladder symptoms
    • Upper or lower midline appendix: Epigastric or hypogastric pain
    • Situs inversus: When present, pain is located at the LLQ
