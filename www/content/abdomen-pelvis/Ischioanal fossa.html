Ischioanal fossa

Location and shape

On the lateral part of the anal canal

Shape - pyramidal / wedge shaped

Apex - upwards and base - downwards

Boundaries

Anterior - posterior border of urogenital diaphragm

Posterior - lower border of gluteus maximus and sacrotuberous ligament

Medial - levator ani and external anal sphincter

Lateral - obturator internus and its fascia with ischial tuberosity below

Superior - apex is formed where obturator fascia meets the inferior layer of urogenital diaphragm

Inferior - floor is by perineal skin

Contents

  • Ischiorectal pad of fat
  • Inferior rectal nerve and vessels
  • Perineal branch of S4
  • Branches of posterior cutaneous nerve of thigh

Spaces around the ischioanal fossa

Ischioanal space proper

Region between perianal fascia and lunate fascia

Perianal space

Fat filled area between the skin and perianal fascia

Infection of this space - leads to perianal abscess

Suprategmental space

Space between the lunate fascia and apex of ischioanal fossa

Lunate fascia

Begins laterally at pudendal canal & merges medially with fascia covering deep part of external anal sphincter

Hiatus of Schwalbe

A gap between the tendinous origin of levator ani & obturator fascia is called Hiatus of Schwalbe.

Pelvic organs may herniate through this gap causing ischiorectal hernia

Pudendal canal = Alcock's canal

Contents of pudendal canal

Pudendal nerve

Internal pudendal vessels

Clinical importance - Ischioanal abscess

 Infection of ischioanal fossa

Abscess usually has horshoe shape behind the anal canal

Goodsall's rule

Rule states that

  • External opening anterior to a transverse line drawn across the anal verge is associated with a straight radial tract into the canal,
  • External opening posterior to the transverse line follows a curved fistulous tract to the posterior midline rectal lumen

The fibres of levator ani arrangement is responsible for such a peculiar drainage pattern of these anal fistulas
