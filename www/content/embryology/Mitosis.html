Mitosis

Mitosis is the process of somatic cell division that produces two genetically identical daughter cells from a single parent cell, maintaining chromosome number (2n â†’ 2n). It ensures growth, tissue repair, and asexual reproduction; errors can lead to aneuploidy in cancer.

Stages of Mitosis

Mitosis occurs in four sequential stages, followed by cytokinesis:

  1. Prophase: Chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes (each with two sister chromatids); nucleoli disappear; mitotic spindle forms from centrosomes; nuclear envelope fragments.
  2. Metaphase: Chromosomes align at the metaphase plate (equator); spindle fibers attach to kinetochores on centromeres.
  3. Anaphase: Centromeres split; sister chromatids separate and migrate to opposite poles via shortening microtubules (anaphase A) and poleward spindle elongation (anaphase B).
  4. Telophase: Chromatids reach poles, decondense into chromatin; nuclear envelopes reform; nucleoli reappear.

Cytokinesis: Cytoplasm divides via contractile ring (animal cells) or cell plate (plants), yielding two diploid daughter cells.

Key Features

  • Duration: ~1 hour in actively dividing cells.
  • Regulated by cyclin B-CDK1 complex; checkpoints ensure fidelity.

Mnemonic: PMAT (Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase).

Clinical Relevance: Faulty mitosis causes chromosomal instability in tumors; drugs like vincristine disrupt spindles for chemotherapy.
