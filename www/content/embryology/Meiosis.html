Meiosis

Meiosis is a reductive division in germ cells producing four genetically diverse haploid gametes (2n â†’ n), essential for sexual reproduction and genetic variation via recombination and independent assortment.

Stages of Meiosis

Divided into Meiosis I (reductional) and Meiosis II (equational, similar to mitosis).

Meiosis I:

    • Prophase I: Longest phase; chromosomes pair (synapsis) forming tetrads; crossing over at chiasmata exchanges genetic material (recombination).
    • Metaphase I: Tetrads align at equator; homologous chromosomes attach to spindle.
    • Anaphase I: Homologs separate to poles (no centromere split), reducing ploidy.
    • Telophase I: Nuclei reform; cytokinesis yields two haploid cells.

Prophase I

Prophase I is the longest phase of meiosis (~90% of total time), crucial for genetic recombination. It consists of five sequential sub-stages, enabling synapsis and crossing over between homologous chromosomes.

  1. Leptotene: Chromosomes condense and become visible as thin threads; axial elements form (initiation of synaptonemal complex); no pairing yet.
  2. Zygotene: Homologous chromosomes pair via synapsis, forming synaptonemal complex; bouquet stage (telomeres cluster at nuclear envelope).
  3. Pachytene: Full synapsis complete; tetrads (bivalents) form; crossing over occurs at chiasmata via recombination nodules.
  4. Diplotene: Synaptonemal complex disassembles; homologs separate slightly but held by chiasmata; chromosomes thicken.
  5. Diakinesis: Chromosomes further condense and shorten; chiasmata terminalize; nuclear envelope begins to break down.

Mnemonic: "Lazy Zippers Pack Dirty Diapers" (Leptotene, Zygotene, Pachytene, Diplotene, Diakinesis).

Significance: Ensures genetic diversity; errors lead to aneuploidy (e.g., Klinefelter syndrome).

Meiosis II:

    • Prophase II: Chromosomes recondense; new spindles form.
    • Metaphase II: Chromosomes align singly at equator.
    • Anaphase II: Sister chromatids separate to poles.
    • Telophase II: Nuclei reform; cytokinesis produces four haploid gametes (spermatocytes or ova).

Key Features

  • Genetic variation: Crossing over (30-50/chromosome) + independent assortment (2^n possibilities).
  • In females: Unequal cytokinesis forms one ovum + polar bodies.
  • Regulated by cyclins and checkpoints; errors cause nondisjunction (e.g., Down syndrome).

Clinical Relevance: Meiotic errors lead to aneuploidy (e.g., trisomy 21); infertility treatments target meiotic defects.
