Development of the Liver

Origin of the Hepatic Primordium

  • Day 22: liver primordium first appears at the superior intestinal portal, caudal and ventral to the heart
  • Around the middle of the 3rd week / beginning of the 4th week: the liver bud, gallbladder, and biliary duct arise as a ventral outgrowth from the distal foregut
  • The hepatic diverticulum grows into the transverse septum — a mesodermal structure between the pericardial cavity and the yolk sac stalk, containing the vitelline and umbilical veins

Invasion Pattern — Unique to Humans

  • In humans, endodermal invasion is not by cords of cells (as in most animals) but by migration of individual endoderm cells
  • These cells do not maintain contact with each other — they mingle freely with mesenchymal cells of the transverse septum
  • This individual cell migration pattern means early human hepatogenesis is more dispersed and interactive with the mesenchyme

Human-specific: A higher proportion of umbilical blood is directed to the liver and less is shunted through the ductus venosus in the human fetus than in other animals — suggesting greater fetal hepatic metabolic activity in humans

Biliary System Development

  • The bile duct forms from connecting elements between the hepatic diverticulum and the duodenum → later produces the cystic duct and gallbladder
  • Intrahepatic bile ducts: long assumed to develop by extension of extrahepatic ducts — now known to differentiate from hepatic cells and join the extrahepatic system secondarily
  • Intrahepatic ducts appear first at the hilum and spread peripherally
  • Bile duct maturation process is continuous and active up to term but slows between the 20th and 32nd week (when intraportal granulopoiesis is active — Sergi et al.)
  • Occlusion and recanalization occur in the extrahepatic biliary system — but these processes are not responsible for extrahepatic biliary atresia (a key conceptual point)

Haemopoiesis

  • Earliest blood source: mesoderm of the yolk sac → blood islands → primitive erythrocytes (nucleated, synthesise haemoglobin)
  • Stem cells from blood islands seed the liver and proliferate
  • Liver produces adult-type RBCs, granulocytes, and platelets between 9th and 24th weeks
  • With ossification and bone marrow development, liver haemopoiesis shuts down before birth
  • The potential for hepatic haemopoiesis persists into adult life — expressed in extramedullary haemopoiesis when bone marrow fails (myelofibrosis, thalassaemia major, etc.)

Lobar Asymmetry & Arterial Changes

  • Initially: right lobe is smaller than left lobe
  • Between birth and adulthood: right lobe grows at the expense of the left, which undergoes peripheral degeneration
  • This shift is accompanied by arterial remodelling:
    • In newborns: hepatic artery is the largest branch of the celiac trunk
    • In adults: splenic artery is larger than the hepatic artery
  • The liver mass reduces and shifts rightward → hepatic artery and celiac trunk adopt a left-to-right orientation

Mesenchymal origin of liver stroma:

  • Future hepatic stroma, haemopoietic Kupffer cells, and vessels are all of mesenchymal origin
  • Only hepatocytes and biliary epithelium are endodermal

Molecular Basis

Hepatic specification from foregut endoderm:

  • FGF1/2 from cardiac mesoderm — the first and most critical hepatic inducing signal; FGF signalling from the developing heart induces hepatic fate in adjacent foregut endoderm
  • BMP2/4 from septum transversum mesenchyme — acts synergistically with FGF to specify hepatic endoderm; BMP alone is insufficient
  • FOXA1/2 (HNF3α/β) — pioneer transcription factors; open compacted chromatin at hepatic gene loci, making the foregut endoderm "competent" to respond to FGF/BMP signals before specification occurs
  • GATA4/6 — co-operate with FOXA factors in establishing hepatic competence

Hepatocyte vs. biliary fate decision:

  • Notch signalling (via Jagged1/Notch2) — drives biliary epithelial cell (cholangiocyte) fate; Jagged1 mutations → Alagille syndrome (paucity of intrahepatic bile ducts)
  • TGF-β — promotes biliary differentiation in periportal hepatoblasts
  • SOX9 — expressed in ductal plate cells; marks biliary progenitors; mutations cause various ductal plate malformation syndromes
  • YAP (Hippo pathway effector) — controls liver size; YAP activation drives hepatocyte proliferation; critical for the massive growth of the fetal liver (10% of body volume at 9 weeks)

Haemopoiesis:

  • SCF/c-Kit, CXCL12/CXCR4 — govern homing and expansion of haemopoietic stem cells in the fetal liver
  • EPO (Erythropoietin) — drives fetal hepatic erythropoiesis; EPO is produced by the fetal liver (not kidney, as in adults) until late gestation

Clinical Correlations

Biliary Atresia

  • Progressive inflammatory obliteration of the extrahepatic bile ducts; most common cause of neonatal cholestasis and the most common indication for paediatric liver transplantation
  • Not caused by failure of recanalization — this is explicitly stated (Skandalakis); likely an immune-mediated destruction post-normal formation, possibly triggered by perinatal viral infection (Reovirus 3, CMV) in genetically susceptible individuals
  • Presents: conjugated jaundice beyond 2 weeks, pale stools, dark urine, hepatomegaly
  • Diagnosis: HIDA scan (no excretion into gut) + liver biopsy (bile duct proliferation, fibrosis) + intraoperative cholangiogram
  • Treatment: Kasai portoenterostomy (ideally before 60 days) → ~50% achieve bile drainage; liver transplant for failures

Alagille Syndrome

  • JAG1 mutations (Jagged1, Notch ligand) → failure of biliary differentiation → paucity of intrahepatic bile ducts
  • Features: cholestasis, peripheral pulmonary stenosis, butterfly vertebrae, posterior embryotoxon (eye), characteristic facies
  • Autosomal dominant; 94% penetrance but variable expressivity
  • Directly reflects failure of Notch-mediated cholangiocyte specification

Extramedullary Haemopoiesis

  • Hepatic haemopoiesis (9th–24th week) can be reactivated in adults when bone marrow fails
  • Seen in: myelofibrosis, severe haemolytic anaemias (thalassaemia major, sickle cell), bone marrow infiltration
  • Manifests as hepatosplenomegaly; rarely causes mass lesions

Hepatoblastoma

  • Most common primary liver malignancy in children (<5 years)
  • Recapitulates fetal hepatoblast differentiation — contains both hepatocytic and biliary elements
  • Associated with Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome (IGF2 overexpression, 11p15 imprinting defect) and familial adenomatous polyposis (APC mutation — Wnt pathway)
  • AFP markedly elevated — reflects fetal liver protein expression

Congenital Portosystemic Shunts (Abernethy Malformation)

  • Failure of normal portal venous development → portal blood shunted directly into systemic circulation
  • Type 1 (complete): no intrahepatic portal flow → liver hypoplasia; associated with cardiac anomalies, biliary atresia
  • Type 2 (partial): side-to-side shunt; some intrahepatic flow preserved
  • Can cause hepatic encephalopathy, pulmonary hypertension, hepatopulmonary syndrome

Riedel's Lobe

  • Tongue-like inferior extension of the right lobe — a normal variant, not pathological
  • Reflects asymmetric right lobe growth that continues postnatally; more common in women
  • Can be mistaken for hepatomegaly or a right upper quadrant mass

Key Points to Remember

  • Hepatic primordium = Day 22; hepatic diverticulum growing into transverse septum = Day 24 — differentiation begins before the primordium is recognisable
  • Human endodermal invasion is by individual cell migration, not cords — unique among vertebrates
  • FGF (from heart) + BMP (from septum transversum) = the two essential hepatic induction signals; FOXA1/2 establishes competence before induction
  • Intrahepatic bile ducts differentiate from hepatocytes and grow hilum → periphery; not by extension of extrahepatic ducts
  • Biliary atresia is not a recanalization failure — it is post-formational inflammatory destruction
  • Left umbilical vein persists → round ligament; right regresses at 6th week
  • Liver haemopoiesis: 9th–24th week; EPO produced by fetal liver (not kidney)
  • At 9 weeks: liver = 10% body volume (haemopoiesis + multiple sinusoids); at birth = 5% body weight
  • Right lobe is initially smaller than left; reverses after birth
  • Hepatic artery is largest celiac branch in newborns; splenic artery becomes larger in adults
