Development of Diaphragm

Embryonic Components

  • Septum transversum (ventral mesoderm):
    • Forms the central tendon (non-muscular part).
    • Originates cranially (cervical region) between the heart and liver in week 3–4.
    • Provides the scaffold for myoblast migration and forms the fibrous central part.
  • Pleuroperitoneal membranes (folds) (paired, dorsolateral):
    • Arise from the body wall in late week 4.
    • Grow medially and ventrally to fuse with the septum transversum and dorsal mesentery.
    • Close the pleuroperitoneal canals (pericardioperitoneal canals).
    • Form the major muscular portions of the diaphragm (except crura and periphery).
    • Right fold closes earlier than left (explains why left-sided defects are more common).
  • Dorsal mesentery of esophagus:
    • Forms the crura of the diaphragm (right and left crus).
    • Surrounds the esophagus, aorta, and inferior vena cava at the hiatuses.
  • Muscular ingrowth from lateral body wall (somitic mesoderm from cervical somites C3–C5):
    • Provides the peripheral muscular rim attached to the ribs and costal cartilages.
    • Myoblasts migrate into the other three components, bringing their nerve supply.

Sequence of Development & Descent

  • Week 4: Septum transversum lies at cervical level (C3–C5). Pleuroperitoneal folds appear. Pericardioperitoneal canals are open.
  • Weeks 5–6: Pleuroperitoneal membranes grow and fuse, closing the canals → separation of pleural and peritoneal cavities is complete.
  • Weeks 6–8: Differential growth of the embryo (rapid caudal growth) causes apparent descent of the diaphragm from cervical to thoraco-lumbar level (final position opposite T8–T12 vertebrae).

Phrenic nerve (derived from C3–C5 ventral rami) is carried downward with the descending diaphragm.

Innervation

  • Motor and sensory supply to the entire diaphragm (except peripheral sensory from intercostal nerves) - phrenic nerve (C3, C4, C5)

Explains: Referred pain to shoulder tip (supraclavicular nerves C3–C4).

Hiatuses (formed during fusion)

  • Caval opening (T8) – IVC.
  • Esophageal hiatus (T10) – esophagus + vagi.
  • Aortic hiatus (T12) – aorta, thoracic duct, azygos vein.

Clinical Correlations

  • Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia (CDH): Failure of pleuroperitoneal membrane fusion.
    • Bochdalek hernia (posterolateral, 85–90%): Mostly left-sided → abdominal contents herniate into thorax → pulmonary hypoplasia, scaphoid abdomen, respiratory distress at birth.
    • Morgagni hernia (anterior/retrosternal): Smaller, often right-sided, later presentation.
  • Eventration of diaphragm: Incomplete muscularization → thin, elevated hemidiaphragm.
  • Phrenic nerve injury (birth trauma, surgery): Unilateral paralysis → elevated hemidiaphragm, dyspnea.
  • Hiatal hernia (congenital/acquired): Enlarged esophageal hiatus.
