Meckel's Diverticulum

Meckel's diverticulum results from persistence of the proximal (ileal) end of the vitelline duct - specifically the base of the vitelline stalk where it attaches to the ileum.

Types of ectopic tissue

2 main types — gastric (most common), pancreatic

Limitations of the Rule of 2s:

  • Distance from ICV is highly variable — ranges from 10 cm to 100 cm in studies; the "2 feet" is a teaching approximation
  • Only ~4% of Meckel's diverticula ever become symptomatic — the majority are silent incidental findings

Ectopic Tissues

  • Gastric mucosa (~60% of ectopic tissue cases) — most clinically significant
  • Pancreatic tissue (~15%)
  • Duodenal mucosa, colonic mucosa, hepatobiliary tissue — rare
  • Occasionally a combination of tissues

Why ectopic tissue? The vitelline duct endoderm retains pluripotent endodermal character

Clinical Correlations

Haemorrhage (most common complication in children)

  • Ectopic gastric mucosa secretes HCl → peptic ulceration of adjacent ileal mucosa → painless rectal bleeding

Inflammation (Meckel's Diverticulitis)

  • Clinically indistinguishable from acute appendicitis — right iliac fossa pain, fever, raised WCC
  • Can perforate → peritonitis
  • Rule: if appendix is normal at surgery for suspected appendicitis, always check for Meckel's diverticulum

Littre's Hernia

  • Meckel's diverticulum found within an inguinal, femoral, or umbilical hernia sac

Incidental Meckel's — to remove or not?

  • Current consensus: prophylactic diverticulectomy is reasonable when found incidentally in young patients with high-risk features; individualized decision in older patients

Key Points to Remember

  • True diverticulum — contains all layers of the intestinal wall (mucosa, submucosa, muscularis, serosa)
  • Supplied by a branch of the SMA (a remnant of the right vitelline artery) — confirming midgut origin
  • The mesodermal component governs regression — not an intrinsic property of the endoderm
  • Ectopic gastric mucosa → acid → ulceration of adjacent ileal wall (not within the diverticulum itself)
  • Meckel's scan detects gastric mucosa, not the diverticulum itself — negative scan does not exclude Meckel's
  • Littre's hernia = Meckel's in a hernia sac — the only hernia named after the contents, not the anatomical site
  • CDX2 failure / SOX2 persistence in the remnant most likely explains ectopic gastric differentiation
