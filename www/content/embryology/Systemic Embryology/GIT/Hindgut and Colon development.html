The Cloaca & Its Division

The cloaca is an endodermal sac receiving the hindgut dorsally and the allantoic stalk ventrally

Separated from the outside by the cloacal membrane — a bilaminar membrane (no mesoderm) on the embryo's ventral surface between the tail and body stalk

Urorectal septum — formed by fusion of Tourneux fold (craniocaudal) and Rathke's folds (lateral) — divides the cloaca into:

  • Ventral: urogenital sinus → bladder, urethra, lower vagina
  • Dorsal: rectum + upper anal canal

Urorectal septum fuses with the cloacal membrane → forms the perineal body

Cloacal membrane now divided into:

  • Larger ventral urogenital membrane
  • Smaller dorsal anal membrane

Anal membrane ruptures — leaves no trace

The pectinate line is traditionally considered to correspond to the level of the ruptured anal membrane — though direct evidence is limited

Key point: The external anal sphincter forms at the correct anatomical location even if the rectum ends blindly or opens at an ectopic site — explaining why in anorectal malformations, a sphincter is always present and can be used for reconstruction, regardless of where the bowel ends.

The rectum has the highest follicular density — clinically relevant for lymphoid hyperplasia mimicking polyps or IBD on endoscopy

ENS migration — always craniocaudal:

  • Ganglion cells reach the colon by the 7th week and complete innervation by the 12th week
  • ENS migration is strictly craniocaudal — this is why Hirschsprung disease always involves the rectum and extends proximally, never affecting only a proximal segment while sparing the rectum
  • RET/GDNF — primary axis; RET on neural crest cells, GDNF from gut mesenchyme
  • EDNRB/EDN3 — especially important for the terminal colon and rectum; explains why EDNRB mutations give longer-segment Hirschsprung
  • SOX10, PHOX2B — transcription factors in migrating neural crest cells required for ENS identity
  • L1CAM — cell adhesion molecule required for ENS migration; mutations → X-linked Hirschsprung

Colonic villi — a unique fetal structure:

  • Villi appear in the 3rd month, peak at the 4th month, then regress by the 7th–8th month
  • The mature colon has no villi — their disappearance reflects the shift from absorptive to storage/reservoir function
  • Their presence during fetal life maximises surface area for amniotic fluid absorption

Clinical Correlations

Hirschsprung Disease

  • Ganglion cells normally reach the colon at 7 weeks and complete migration by 12 weeks
  • A developmental arrest at any point → aganglionic segment from that level to the internal sphincter
  • Short segment (rectosigmoid) ~80%; long segment (beyond sigmoid) ~15%; total colonic aganglionosis ~5%
  • Total colonic aganglionosis presents with small bowel dilatation, not just rectal — challenging diagnosis
  • Associated syndromes: Down syndrome (~10% of Hirschsprung), MEN2A (RET mutation shared)

Imperforate Anus

  • Anal membrane fails to rupture by 8th week
  • Low type: anal membrane persists; bowel present at normal position; simple incision curative
  • High type: rectum ends above levator ani; requires posterior sagittal anorectoplasty (PSARP — Peña procedure)
  • Associated with genitourinary anomalies in ~50% — always perform spinal and renal USS
