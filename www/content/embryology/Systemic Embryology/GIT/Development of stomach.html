Development of the Stomach

Formation and initial position

The stomach appears at week 4 as a fusiform dilation of the caudal foregut, initially oriented in the median plane with its long axis craniocaudal.

Rotation and Repositioning

Classical teaching (Moore/Sadler): The stomach undergoes two rotations:

  • Longitudinal (anteroposterior) axis rotation — 90° clockwise:
    • The original dorsal border grows faster → becomes the greater curvature
    • The original ventral border grows slower → becomes the lesser curvature
    • Left vagus (originally posterior) → becomes anterior on the lesser curvature
    • Right vagus (originally anterior) → becomes posterior
  • Craniocaudal axis tilt:
    • Cardia/fundus shift left and downward
    • Pylorus shifts right and upward

Contemporary view (O'Rahilly & Müller): Rather than true rotation, the apparent positional change may be explained entirely by differential growth of the two curvatures — the greater curvature simply grows faster and expands more. There is no compelling histological or imaging evidence for actual axial rotation. This explains the observations just as well without invoking a rotational mechanism.

The sequence — parietal cells before chief cells — reflects the maturation order of the gastric unit. Parietal cells are the first functional secretory cells; HCl secretion begins in fetal life and has a role in amniotic fluid acidification and possible antimicrobial defence.

Molecular Basis

Foregut-stomach specification:

  • SOX2 — expressed in the stomach endoderm; essential for gastric identity. SOX2 suppresses intestinal fate (CDX2). Loss of SOX2 → intestinal metaplasia of the stomach.
  • BARX1 — mesenchymal transcription factor critical for stomach-specific identity; acts by repressing WNT signaling locally in the stomach region. BARX1 knockout mice develop an intestinalized stomach.
  • WNT signaling — suppressed in the stomach region (by BARX1/sFRP); active in the intestine. This WNT suppression/activation boundary defines the gastroduodenal junction.

Gland & cell differentiation:

  • Notch signaling — controls fate decisions between surface mucous cells and glandular cell types (parietal, chief, enteroendocrine)
  • Sonic Hedgehog (SHH) — secreted from gastric endoderm; regulates mesenchymal patterning and gland morphogenesis
  • NGN3 (Neurogenin-3) — required for enteroendocrine cell differentiation in the stomach
  • MIST1 (BHLHA15) — transcription factor required for mature chief cell (zymogenic cell) identity; MIST1 knockout → chief cells fail to fully differentiate
  • H⁺/K⁺-ATPase (ATP4A/ATP4B) — parietal cell proton pump; expressed from ~11th week, reflecting early functional maturation

Curvature/rotation patterning:

  • Pitx2 — expressed asymmetrically on the left side; controls left-right asymmetry including gastric positioning
  • Nodal/Lefty/Pitx2 cascade — the same left-right axis pathway that determines cardiac looping also positions the stomach; mutations cause situs inversus with gastric malposition

Clinical Correlations

Hypertrophic Pyloric Stenosis (HPS)

  • Hypertrophy of the circular muscle layer of the pylorus, manifesting at 2–6 weeks of age
  • Projectile, non-bilious vomiting → metabolic hypochloraemic hypokalaemic alkalosis
  • Pathogenesis involves abnormal NOS (nitric oxide synthase) expression in the pyloric muscle → failure of relaxation; also linked to reduced IGF-1 and EGF signalling in the pyloric wall
  • Associated with erythromycin use in neonates (motilin receptor agonist → pyloric spasm)
  • Treatment: Ramstedt pyloromyotomy

Gastric Volvulus

  • Abnormal rotation or laxity of gastric ligaments → stomach twists on itself
  • Organoaxial (around long axis, most common) vs. mesenteroaxial
  • Embryological basis: abnormal formation or laxity of the gastrosplenic and gastrophrenic ligaments (derivatives of the dorsal mesogastrium)

Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD) in neonates

  • Immaturity of the lower oesophageal sphincter (LOS); the cardia has not fully descended to its subdiaphragmatic position
  • Physiological in early infancy; pathological if associated with failure to thrive or oesophagitis

Congenital Microgastria

  • Rare failure of stomach to expand from its fusiform primordium; presents as severe GERD, failure to thrive, recurrent aspiration
  • Associated with asplenia and limb anomalies

Intestinal Metaplasia of the Stomach

  • Loss of SOX2 expression and gain of CDX2 expression in gastric mucosa → gastric epithelium acquires intestinal characteristics
  • Driven by H. pylori infection, bile reflux
  • Pre-cancerous lesion → gastric adenocarcinoma (intestinal type)
  • Direct embryological relevance: CDX2 normally suppressed in the stomach — its re-expression "reverts" cells to intestinal phenotype

Ectopic Pancreatic Tissue (Heterotopia)

  • Pancreatic rest most commonly found in the stomach (antrum/pylorus), duodenum, or Meckel's diverticulum
  • Results from misdirected differentiation of multipotent endodermal progenitors at the foregut-midgut junction
  • Usually asymptomatic but can cause gastric outlet obstruction or bleeding

Key Points to Remember

  • Differential growth (not rotation) most likely explains curvature formation — O'Rahilly & Müller are likely correct
  • Parietal cells appear before chief cells (~11 vs ~12 weeks) — functional order matters
  • Left vagus = anterior (lesser curvature); Right vagus = posterior — a direct consequence of stomach repositioning
  • SOX2 vs CDX2 balance determines gastric vs intestinal identity — clinically relevant in metaplasia
  • BARX1 is the key mesenchymal factor that makes the stomach a stomach (not intestine)
  • Pyloric stenosis pathogenesis now understood to involve NOS deficiency, not just mechanical hypertrophy
