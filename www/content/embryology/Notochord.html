Formation of Notochord

Notochord is a transient embryonic structure that defines the body axis and induces neural tube formation. It largely degenerates by birth.

Persistent component OF NOTOCHORD, is the nucleus pulposus, the gelatinous, avascular core of intervertebral discs that provides shock absorption and flexibility to the spine—composed of hydrated proteoglycans and type II collagen derived from notochordal cells.

With formation of notochord – anteroposterior axis is fixed.

Notochordal Process

From primitive node, epiblast cells ingress and forms hollow notochordal process (head process rostrally)

This elongates cranially between ectoderm and endoderm (day 16)

Neurenteric plate and canal

Ventral floor merges of notochord merges with endoderm and forms notochordal plate (transient hollow phase).

Neurenteric canal is a brief lumen (day 17-18) bridging amnion above to yolk sac below

Notochordal plate fuses dorsally and forms solid notochord

This induces formation of somites through Noggin/SHH, forms disc cores

Solid Notochord: Canal closes (apoptosis). Notochordal plate condenses to rod (SHH/Brachyury-driven), inducing neural tube/sclerotomes (future vertebrae/discs).

The notochord along with the medial part of the intraembryonic mesoderem represents the future vertebral column.

Clinical relevance

Neural Tube Defects (NTDs): Notochord SHH induction failure lead to anencephaly or spina bifida

Chordomas: Notochord remnants overexpress Brachyury lead to aggressive tumors

Vertebral malformations: Incomplete sclerotome induction (Noggin low) lead to Klippel-Feil syndrome (fused cervical vertebrae)
