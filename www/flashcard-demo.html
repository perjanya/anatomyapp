Cubital Fossa

The cubital fossa is a triangular space on the anterior aspect of the elbow. It contains important neurovascular structures that are clinically relevant for venipuncture and nerve blocks.

The fossa is bounded by the brachioradialis laterally, pronator teres medially, and an imaginary line between the epicondyles superiorly. Its floor is formed by the brachialis and supinator muscles.

Clinical importance: The median cubital vein, commonly used for venipuncture, crosses superficially. Deep needle insertion risks damaging the underlying median nerve and brachial artery.

Shoulder Joint

The glenohumeral joint is a ball-and-socket synovial joint between the head of the humerus and the glenoid fossa of the scapula. It is the most mobile joint in the human body but sacrifices stability for this range of motion.

Stability is provided primarily by the rotator cuff muscles (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis), which surround the joint capsule.

Common injuries: Rotator cuff tears (especially supraspinatus), anterior dislocations (most common type), and impingement syndrome.

Heart Anatomy

The heart is a four-chambered muscular pump located in the middle mediastinum. It consists of two atria (receiving chambers) and two ventricles (pumping chambers).

Blood flow through the heart follows a specific pathway: right atrium ‚Üí right ventricle ‚Üí pulmonary circulation ‚Üí left atrium ‚Üí left ventricle ‚Üí systemic circulation.

Coronary circulation: The heart receives its blood supply from the right and left coronary arteries, which arise from the aortic sinuses immediately above the aortic valve.

Brachial Plexus

The brachial plexus is a network of nerves formed by the ventral rami of spinal nerves C5-T1. It provides motor and sensory innervation to the upper limb.

Organization: Roots ‚Üí Trunks ‚Üí Divisions ‚Üí Cords ‚Üí Branches

Clinical injuries: Erb's palsy (upper trunk injury causing "waiter's tip" position) and Klumpke's palsy (lower trunk injury causing "claw hand").
