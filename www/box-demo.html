Info Box (Blue) - General Information

Anatomical Landmark

The acromion is the lateral extension of the scapula that forms the highest point of the shoulder.

Key Point

The shoulder joint (glenohumeral joint) is a ball-and-socket synovial joint.

Tip Box (Green) - Helpful Tips

Study Tip

Remember the rotator cuff muscles using the mnemonic: SITS (Supraspinatus, Infraspinatus, Teres minor, Subscapularis)

Remember

The musculocutaneous nerve pierces the coracobrachialis muscle.

Warning Box (Orange) - Cautions

Common Mistake

Don't confuse the axillary nerve with the radial nerve. The axillary nerve supplies the deltoid and teres minor.

Note/Important Box (Purple) - Important Points

Important Note

The supraspinatus muscle is most commonly torn in rotator cuff injuries.

Exam Focus

The quadrangular space contains the axillary nerve and posterior circumflex humeral artery.

Clinical Box (Red) - Clinical Significance

Clinical Significance

Injury to the axillary nerve (e.g., from shoulder dislocation) causes paralysis of the deltoid muscle and loss of sensation over the "regimental badge" area.

Definition Box (Gray) - Definitions

Definition: Anastomosis

A connection between two blood vessels that allows blood to flow from one to the other.

Terminology

Abduction: Movement of a limb away from the midline of the body.

Nested Content Example

The Deltoid Muscle

The deltoid has three parts:

  • Anterior: Flexion and medial rotation
  • Middle: Abduction (main action)
  • Posterior: Extension and lateral rotation

All three parts work together during shoulder abduction.
